# Dust and Dead Men: A Stranger Rides Into Dustfall

*Ironsworn: Badlands — Session One*

---

The sun had been at Jake Powell's back for two hours when Wrangler crested the ridge and showed him the town below.

Dustfall. Maybe two hundred souls on a good day — the kind of place that earned its name from the way the alkali flats to the west lit up in late afternoon, everything going the color of old bone. A main street, a water tower, a church with a steeple that listed slightly to the south as though listening for something underground. Cattle pens at the far end, mostly empty.

The smoke was coming from what used to be the livery stable.

Not a fresh fire. Whatever had burned had done its burning the night before, and all that remained now were the black ribs of timber standing against the afternoon sky — a skeleton that had forgotten it was supposed to fall. A thin grey thread rose from the ruins into still air with nowhere particular to go. A small figure stood in front of the wreckage, motionless as a headstone.

Jake was still taking all this in when he noticed the rider coming up the road toward him. An older man, heavyset, moving at a pace that wasn't quite a gallop but wasn't comfortable either. The lawman's star on his chest caught the light once, twice, like a signal mirror. He hadn't spotted Jake yet.

Wrangler shifted his weight beneath his rider, ears pricked forward.

Jake clicked his tongue softly and let the horse pick his way down the slope at a walk. He kept his eyes on the burnt stable as he moved, reading the ruins the way a man reads a face — looking for what they were trying not to show.

---

The lawman pulled up when they were close enough to talk without raising their voices. He read Jake the way all men out here learned to read strangers: a quick inventory, hands to gun to horse to face, and then a decision. Not relaxed — just concluded that Jake wasn't the most urgent problem in his day.

His face was in its fifties, weathered past the point where tired and just-built-that-way were distinguishable from each other. The star on his chest read *Marshal* in letters that had been re-stamped at least once.

"You riding toward Dustfall," he said, "or just riding?"

He asked it the way a man asks when he's hoping the answer saves him a conversation he doesn't have time for.

"Just riding," Jake said. "And Dustfall appears to be on my way."

The marshal looked at him a moment longer than the words required. "Hm." He glanced back over his shoulder — reflex, the way a man keeps looking at a wound — and then turned back. "Name's Calhoun. Marshal of Dustfall." A pause. "For what that's worth this week."

He didn't offer his hand. Not unfriendly. Just a man with too much sitting on top of him for pleasantries.

"You got business in town," Calhoun asked, "or do you just need a bed and a meal?"

There was weight in it. Not accusation — more like he was sorting Jake into a category before deciding how much to say. Down the slope, the small figure by the ruins finally moved: crouched down, picked something from the ash, held it up. Too far to see what.

Calhoun watched Jake watch it.

"That's Marta Reyes," he said, quieter. "It was her stable."

He let that sit between them like something that hadn't finished falling yet.

Jake asked what had happened. Calhoun's mouth tightened.

"Fire," he said. Then, like he knew that wasn't an answer: "In the night. Nobody hurt. Marta got the horses out." He paused. "Third thing this month. Ike Pruitt lost half his fence line two weeks ago — cut clean, not rot, not drift, cut. Week before that, somebody poisoned the well at the Sandoval place. Old couple. Had to haul water from the creek for ten days."

He stopped himself. Seemed to realize he'd said more than he meant to.

"Dustfall's a quiet town," he said. The way a man says something he used to believe.

He looked at Jake the way men look at strangers when they're deciding whether trouble or help just rode in off the flats.

Jake said he'd be happy to keep his eyes open.

Calhoun's eyes narrowed just slightly. "Depends on what you can do," he said. "And who you are." A beat. "I didn't catch a name."

"Jake. This is Wrangler."

The corner of Calhoun's mouth moved — not quite a smile but in the neighborhood. He let the name sit there a moment, turning it over like a coin found in the dirt. "Jake got a second name?"

Then, without waiting: "Powell."

Not a question.

He'd heard it somewhere. Jake could see him placing it, the way a man places a face he can't quite locate — and something in his assessment of the situation adjusted, though his expression gave away nothing beyond the adjustment itself.

"Heard that name," Calhoun said. "Can't remember where."

Which might have been true.

He looked at Jake one more long moment. Then at Marta Reyes, still crouching in the ash of twelve years of work. Then back.

"I've got no budget for a deputy," Calhoun said. "No council backing. And whatever's happening in my town, I can't prove a damn thing yet." He said it evenly, no apology in it. Just facts laid out like cards. "But if a man wanted to ask around, have a drink, keep his eyes open — I wouldn't object to that."

He clicked his tongue and his horse stepped aside, clearing the road.

"Marta makes good coffee," he said. "If she's still speaking to people today."

Jake tipped his hat and rode on down toward Dustfall.

---

The town came up around him gradually, the way small towns always did. A dog that didn't bother barking. A boy on a fence who stared without shame. The smell of dust and livestock and woodsmoke, and underneath it all — faint but persistent — the particular char of last night's fire.

Main street was quiet for mid-afternoon. A few horses at the rail outside the saloon. A woman hanging laundry who watched Jake ride past with the careful eyes of someone who had learned to notice strangers. A man outside the general store stopped sweeping and just held his broom.

Word traveled fast in small towns. Or maybe Dustfall was just jumpy.

The ruins of the livery were at the near end of the street, and up close the damage was worse — the whole interior gutted, the stalls collapsed and black, the smell of it still sharp and ugly. Someone had already stacked the surviving tack and tools in a neat pile under a canvas lean-to. Practical grief, Jake thought. The most honest kind.

Marta Reyes stood with her back to the street, looking into the ruins. She was turning a small iron horseshoe in her hands, soot on her fingers, soot on her boots. She didn't look up at the sound of Wrangler's hooves.

Wrangler stopped of his own accord and snorted.

Jake tipped his hat. "Howdy, ma'am. I'm awful sorry for what appears to have happened here."

She turned.

She had the kind of face that had been pretty once and had stopped caring about it somewhere along the way. Sharp dark eyes that did the same inventory Calhoun had done — faster, and less interested in the gun.

"You just ride in?" she asked.

"Yes ma'am."

She nodded once, like that confirmed something, and looked back at the ruins. "Twelve years I ran this stable. Never had so much as a kicked-over lantern." She said it without particular emotion. The way you state a fact you're still learning the shape of.

She turned the horseshoe over in her hands. It was small — pony-sized, or mule. Bent slightly out of true.

"They didn't have to do it in the night," she said. "That's what I keep thinking. They did it in the night so I couldn't see their faces." She paused. "Which means they live here. Which means I probably know them."

She looked up at Jake for the first time, direct and without apology. "You're not just passing through," she said. "Men just passing through don't stop."

Jake asked about the horses. Something in Marta's wariness dropped a fraction.

"Smoke woke me," she said. She glanced toward the small adobe house set forty feet back from the ruins. "I sleep light since my husband passed. Used to bother me. Doesn't anymore."

She'd made three trips through smoke and heat to save six horses. The last one, she admitted, she'd known she didn't need to make. There was an old mare in the back — belonged to the Sandoval family, boarded because they couldn't work her but couldn't bring themselves to put her down. "Couldn't leave her," Marta said simply.

She held up the bent horseshoe.

"Found this in the ash this morning. Doesn't belong to any of my boarders." She turned it over. "Somebody lost it here who shouldn't have been here. Could be nothing. But it's something to hold onto."

She studied Jake for a moment longer. "You didn't answer my question. About just passing through."

"Just a traveller," he said. "But I think I might stay a bit. Seems interesting."

Something that might have been the distant ancestor of a smile crossed her face and disappeared. "'Interesting,'" she repeated, like she was tasting the word.

She looked at Wrangler with a professional eye — took in his condition, his temperament, the way he was standing — and made him an offer: the corral out back, still standing, a dollar a day with feed. Then, casual as weather: "Marshal talk to you on the road?"

She'd already figured out he had. She was asking what he'd said.

Jake told her. Calhoun had mentioned the strange happenings. And he'd mentioned her coffee.

Marta looked at the ruins of her stable. Then back at Jake. "He's not wrong about the coffee."

She tucked the horseshoe into her apron pocket and jerked her head toward the adobe house. "Leave your horse at the corral post. Gate's around the side."

---

The inside of Marta's house was small and clean and smelled of chili pepper and woodsmoke. A crucifix on the wall. A framed photograph of a broad-shouldered man with a serious face — her husband, clearly, a man who had looked older than his years. A rifle above the door, recently cleaned by the look of it.

She put the coffee on without asking how he took it.

"Pruitt's fence," she said, her back to Jake, working at the stove. "Sandoval's well. My stable." She was quiet for a moment. "You know what those three things have in common?"

She turned and set two cups on the table and sat down across from him. Her hands wrapped around the cup like she was steadying herself against something.

"Garrison Cole has made offers on all three properties," she said. "In the last six months. Pruitt said no. Sandovals said no." A beat. "I said no."

She let that sit.

"Marshal Calhoun knows all of this," she said. "He can't prove anything."

Cole, she explained, was the man the whole town orbited. He ran the bank. Half the town owed him money and the other half worked for him. He sat in the front pew on Sundays and made reasonable offers and smiled his reasonable smile. And then, when the reasonable offers were declined, fences got cut. Wells got poisoned. Stables caught fire in the middle of the night.

The railroad was coming up from Laredo. Everyone had known it was coming; the question had always been where. Six months ago, Cole's people had gotten word of the survey route — had paid for that word, depending on who you asked. And the route ran through property that hadn't been willing to sell.

Marta's land sat right where they needed the depot. Pruitt's fence line ran along the future rail approach. The Sandovals had held their land for forty years under an old Mexican land grant, and Cole wanted them gone before anyone started asking inconvenient questions about right of way.

Six properties had already changed hands in the past year, some from desperation, some from misfortune, at least one or two from the particular kind of misfortune that came with being on Cole's wrong side. In each case the pattern was the same: those who sold quickly came through unscathed; those who held out found that bad things had a way of finding them.

"He's never in the room when it happens," Marta said. "Never will be."

A fly landed on the table between them. She didn't move to chase it.

Jake asked about the timing — the fire had come faster than the previous incidents. Something in Marta's eyes shifted.

"Which makes me think something changed," she said. "Some deadline I don't know about." She pulled the bent horseshoe from her apron pocket and set it on the table. "He's more impatient now. There's a reason for that and I don't know what it is yet."

She looked at Jake with the steady eyes of a woman who had done her crying and moved on to something harder.

"I'm not a woman who asks for help easily," she said. "But I'm not a fool either." She wrapped her hands around her cup. "I can't pay you. Not right now. But I can board your horse for free as long as you're here. I can feed you." She paused. "And when I rebuild—" she caught herself, corrected it— "*when* I rebuild, there's something to be said for having done right by someone when they needed it."

She met his eyes.

"I'm asking if you'll look into it," she said. "That's all. Just look."

Jake stood up from the table. He was already going to look, he said. He'd told the marshal as much. He thought he'd ride out and pay Ike Pruitt a visit.

Something genuine crossed Marta's face and vanished.

She walked him to the door, and as he stepped back out into the afternoon light she gave him two things: directions to Pruitt's place — *follow the main road until you see a water tower with a bullet hole in it, his lane goes left* — and a name to watch out for. "Cole's foreman is a man named Dix," she said. "Younger than you'd expect. Quiet." She said it the way you'd describe weather about to turn. Then she stepped back inside.

Jake took the bent horseshoe she pressed into his hand. Iron, still faintly warm from her fingers.

He rode north.

---

The road out of Dustfall was good hardpack, straight as intention. The alkali flats opened up to the west and the light was getting that late afternoon quality — long shadows, everything gone gold and slightly unreal. Wrangler settled into an easy walk, ears relaxed, the way a good horse carries himself when the road is clear and the rider's mind is elsewhere.

Four miles went quickly in good weather.

The water tower appeared on the horizon before the ranch did. Sure enough — a hole punched through the tank maybe eight feet up, the edges rust-stained from years of slow weeping. Old damage. The lane left was rutted and dry. Half a mile in, a big brindle dog announced their arrival, all bark and no commitment, circling at a distance Wrangler completely ignored.

The ranch was modest but not neglected. A weathered house with solid bones. A barn that had seen better decades but been maintained with genuine care. The cut fence line was visible to the east — maybe two hundred yards of posts standing naked, the wire gone, cattle trails drifting through the gap toward a neighbor's land that probably wasn't happy about the company.

A man was restringing the wire alone. Big through the shoulders, moving with the deliberate economy of someone who had done hard work for so long it had simply become the way he occupied the world. He hadn't looked up.

But he knew they were there.

"Howdy," Jake said. "Looks like you need a hand."

Pruitt straightened slowly. He had twenty years on Jake at least, maybe more. Big hands, one of them wrapped in a rag that had been white that morning. A face like the country — hard used, not unpleasant, not welcoming. He looked at Jake the way he probably looked at weather. Assessing.

"I've been stringing fence since before you were born," he said. "Don't need a hand."

He went back to work.

Jake dismounted, trusted Wrangler to find a patch of scrub grass and stay out of trouble, and walked over and started helping without another word.

They worked in silence for a time — the good kind of silence, the kind that develops between people doing physical work together, finding each other's rhythm. Pruitt was strong and knew what he was doing. Jake matched his pace without trying to lead it. The clean cuts in the old wire spoke for themselves: this had been deliberate work, done in the dark by someone patient and unhurried who knew exactly what they were about.

After maybe ten minutes, Pruitt spoke without looking up. "Who told you."

Not a question. "Marta or Calhoun," he said. "Those are the only two who'd send somebody. Or you're from Cole —" a sideways glance, brief and sharp— "which I don't think you are."

Jake told him about the road, about the coffee. Pruitt absorbed this and kept working. He'd heard about the fire already — word had come from a boy on the road that morning. His first question was whether Marta had gotten the horses out. He asked it like it mattered to him, which it clearly did, even if he'd never have said so directly.

"Good," he said, when Jake confirmed it. Like the word cost him something and he was paying it anyway.

They worked another stretch. The shadows were getting longer. A hawk worked the thermals above the flats, patient and unhurried, its wide circles a kind of commentary on the pace of human trouble.

"Marta talks when she trusts somebody," Pruitt said eventually. Another sideways look. "You got a face that invites confession, or did something happen."

Jake showed him the horseshoe.

Pruitt took it. Didn't look at it right away — finished the staple he was working on first, like a man who won't be rushed by anything less than fire. Then he straightened and held the iron up in the fading light, turning it over in his hands with a slowness that was actually close attention.

"Marta found this in the ash?"

He tilted it, ran his thumb along the inside curve.

"This isn't my work," he said. "I roll my toes different. Flatter." He kept looking at it. "This is a particular style. More pronounced curve on the inside branch." He turned it again. "There's a man shoes horses for Cole's outfit. Comes through every six weeks or so, does the whole remuda at once. Name's Briggs. Travels a circuit, works three or four ranches." He handed the horseshoe back. "This looks like his work."

He was careful about what that meant, and what it didn't. A lost shoe could have been thrown days earlier, carried anywhere. It wasn't proof. But it was a thread.

"Briggs is due in this area again in about a week," Pruitt said. "Maybe less."

The light was going fast now — that quick western collapse from gold to purple that happened so suddenly it always caught a man slightly off guard. Pruitt looked at the remaining fence line and made the arithmetic of the situation clear in a single word: "No."

They wound up the remaining wire. He told Jake he could sleep in the barn, then headed toward the house. "Briggs," he said, without preamble, as an afterthought that wasn't quite. "When he comes through — look at his horses. See if any are throwing a shoe on the left foreleg." He glanced back. "That horseshoe Marta found. It's off a left foreleg."

Supper's in an hour. He left it there.

---

The barn was old but honest. Three stalls, two of them occupied — a grey gelding who regarded Wrangler with mild curiosity and an older bay mare who had apparently decided that regarding anything was beneath her current dignity. Jake worked through the evening routine with the quiet attention of a man at peace with repetitive things: saddle off, rubdown, water, hay. The kind of work that didn't need thinking, just hands.

The last light died out of the sky. Through the barn slats, the stars began their nightly work, appearing one by one over the alkali flats like they were deciding whether to bother.

Supper at Pruitt's table was beans and cornbread and salt pork, and coffee so strong it had opinions of its own. They ate in the good silence again. Outside, the stars had committed fully to the undertaking.

Pruitt poured more coffee without asking.

"You been to Calvert," he said. Not a question.

He kept eating. Not looking at Jake. The way a man brings something up when he wants to discuss it but refuses to be accused of prying. He'd heard different things about Calvert, he said — different things depending on who was telling it. Some said a man rode in and sorted out a bad situation without anybody getting killed. Some said differently. He didn't need to know which version was true.

Jake told him he got that a lot, and that none of the stories were probably exactly right.

Pruitt nodded once, the nod of a man who'd asked a question, received an answer that wasn't quite an answer, and had decided that was answer enough. "Fair," he said.

He drank his coffee. The lamp flickered in some hidden draft. Outside, a coyote started up in the middle distance, that lonesome sound, then thought better of it and stopped.

"Cole's going to come at me again," Pruitt said, not like a man afraid but like a man reading weather. "Fence being cut didn't move me. He'll know that by now." He looked at his plate. "Next thing will be worse."

He'd started the year with thirty-one head. He had twenty-three left, after the cattle that had drifted through the gap. His neighbor Henderson had been decent about returning strays without charging — but Henderson did business with Cole's bank, and that decency had its own quiet expiration date.

He looked up at Jake. "I'm not selling." Same words Marta had used. Same tone. Same door closing.

He asked Jake's read on it.

Jake told him plainly: he'd seen this kind of thing before. It got worse. Simple, honest folk like him and Marta tended to get rolled over. He didn't like it, and when he didn't like something, he had a habit of sticking his nose in.

Pruitt looked at him for a long moment. Then he leaned back slightly in his chair — the closest thing to ease Jake had seen from him — and asked whether Calhoun knew he was out here. He was mapping the pieces. Then, without waiting for the answer: Cole's foreman Dix ran three men who did the particular kind of work that couldn't be proven. Two of them were brothers, the Caulder boys — mean in the specific way of men who had been given permission to be. They were the ones who had cut his fence. He couldn't prove it, but he knew it the way you knew weather.

Cole himself, Pruitt said carefully, was not stupid. Came to church. Sat in the front pew. Made reasonable loans at fair rates and maybe genuinely believed the railroad was good for the territory, and maybe he wasn't even wrong about that part. "He just also believes that what he wants is the same thing as what's right," Pruitt said. "Men like that are more dangerous than men who know they're doing wrong."

He finished his coffee. "You're going to need to watch yourself around Dix," he said quietly. "He'll know about you by morning."

Jake had seen the eyes watching him as he rode through town. He said so. He'd expected Cole to hear soon enough.

Then he set his cup down and looked at Pruitt directly.

"I'm going to help you and Marta," he said. "And I guess Calhoun at the same time. I don't like big men throwing their weight around. It might get messy. But I mean to see you keep your ranch and Marta keep her stable. You good with that?"

The lamp flickered. The coyote had started up again somewhere further off, moving away.

Pruitt was quiet for a long moment. He looked at Jake with the eyes of a man who had learned not to trust offers easily, and who was fighting that learning right now with everything he had.

"I'm good with that," he said. Simply. Finally.

He refilled both cups without being asked.

---

Jake extended his hand across the table. Pruitt took it. One shake — firm, unhurried, the kind that didn't need anything added to it. The kind that was a weight placed on the scale of the world.

It settled on Jake the way such commitments always did. Not heavy exactly. Just present. Like something decided had been given its name at last.

Pruitt released his hand and looked at him steadily.

"There's something I didn't tell Calhoun," he said. "Something I haven't told anyone."

He turned his coffee cup in his hands.

"Three weeks ago I found a man on my land. Dead. Shot once, close range. Young man — didn't recognize him." He looked up. "I buried him out by the south pasture. Didn't report it."

The lamp flickered again.

"He was carrying papers," Pruitt said. "Survey papers. With Cole's name on them." He met Jake's eyes. "I kept them."

He was quiet a moment. "I didn't know what to do with that information. Maybe you do."

Jake took the papers. He asked the natural questions: whether Cole knew they were missing, and why the young man had ended up on Pruitt's land specifically.

Pruitt had been working through it. The survey route ran through his north pasture — maybe the dead man had been walking the line. Doing his job. Wrong place, wrong time. Or someone had followed him there specifically because it was remote.

As for whether Cole knew about the papers — the fence had been cut about a week after Pruitt found the body. "Could be coincidence," he said, in a way that made clear he didn't believe in that particular coincidence. "Could be somebody saw me find him. Could be they searched the body after, found the papers missing, and started working backwards."

He rubbed the back of his neck.

"If Cole knows I have them," he said quietly, "I'm not just a holdout rancher anymore. I'm a problem that needs solving a different way than cutting fence wire." He looked at Jake directly. "Which might be why you showing up today felt like either very good timing, or very bad."

---

The lamp was burning low. Outside, Dustfall was four miles away and dark.

Jake rose from the table and thanked Pruitt for supper. New light in the morning, he said, and he'd start getting some answers.

Pruitt nodded. He didn't try to fill the silence. He picked up the plates without ceremony, and as he stood at the washbasin with his back to Jake, he said one more thing: "Those papers — be careful who sees them. Cole's got eyes in Dustfall I haven't identified yet. Might be more than one."

That was all. Just that.

---

The barn was cool and dark and smelled like honest work. Wrangler shifted when Jake came in, offered a soft greeting, then settled again. Jake climbed to the loft. The hay was good and dry. Through a gap in the roof boards, the stars were doing their indifferent, permanent work overhead.

He held the oilskin packet in his hands for a moment. He could study the papers by lantern light. Let the whole picture settle in tonight while the ranch breathed around him.

He set them aside. Whatever they said would still be true in the morning. He was usefully tired — the good kind, the kind that came from a day of riding and listening and paying attention — and sleep came quickly.

Wrangler shifted once in the small hours. Something moving in the darkness outside — a coyote working the perimeter, testing its luck. Then quiet again.

The stars wheeled overhead through the gap in the roof.

---

Pruitt was true to his word. The smell of coffee reached the barn before full light. The sky outside had gone from black to the deep blue-grey that preceded the sun's commitment to the day. Jake came down from the loft and settled on a hay bale with the oilskin packet and the early grey light coming clean through the barn slats.

The papers were professional work. Clean draftsmanship, careful notation. The kind of survey that cost real money to commission — and the kind that, once read, could not be unread.

Pruitt had been right about the depot location. West side. Clear as anything. Marta's land. The Sandoval place. Pruitt's north pasture. The rail line didn't go near Cole's bank at all.

Jake folded the papers carefully back into the oilskin and sat with the weight of what he now knew.

Cole had been lying to the whole town about where the railroad was going. Everyone who had sold on the east side had sold for nothing. Everyone holding out on the west side was sitting on exactly the land Cole needed — land he was trying to acquire through patience and politeness and, when those failed, through fire and poison and the kind of men who did their work in darkness.

And somewhere in Pruitt's north pasture, under four feet of dry earth, lay a young surveyor who had known the truth and would not be telling it to anyone.

The coffee smell drifted from the house.

Jake tucked the oilskin into his coat and stood up. Outside, through the barn slats, the first real light of morning was beginning to touch the alkali flats to the west — turning everything briefly, briefly gold before the long hard day began in earnest.

Dustfall was four miles south.

It was time to get some answers.

---

*The vow is sworn. The papers are in Jake's coat. And somewhere in Dustfall, Garrison Cole is about to learn that a stranger rode in yesterday and didn't ride out.*
