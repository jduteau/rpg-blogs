# Finding Out What's What

*Session 0 — The Caves of Chaos Campaign*

---

It was late afternoon in early autumn when the five of them crested the last hill and saw Hommlet below.

The village lay scattered across the valley floor like something a child had set down and forgotten — thatched roofs going gold in the low sun, a mill wheel turning lazily in the stream, smoke rising from chimneys in the calm, unhurried way of a place that had not yet heard bad news today. The smell of it reached them before the sound: woodsmoke, manure, and something good frying in fat. An ordinary village at the end of an ordinary harvest.

Brother Aldric fell into step beside Thomas Powell, his boots raising small puffs of dust from the dry road. "At least it has an inn," the old cleric said. He had the particular gift of making observations that sounded casual but were in fact requirements.

From somewhere near the rear, Lance said nothing. He was watching the treeline. Old habit. Nothing moved.

They could see the shape of the place from up here: the inn and its stable yard, the mill, a modest chapel with a freshly painted symbol above the door, and — on the far side of the village, catching the last of the sun — a new tower under construction. Stone work, recently mortared, scaffolding still climbing its upper half. Someone in Hommlet was building for the future.

Thomas studied it for a moment, then looked at the chapel. "Aldric. With me."

Conrad's expression said *don't be long.* He followed Michael and Lance down toward the warm glow of the inn.

---

## The Welcome Wench

The common room of the Welcome Wench was everything such rooms were meant to be: a long bar, a fire going, and perhaps a dozen locals settled into the comfortable noise of early evening. The innkeeper — a broad man named Gundigoot, with the professionally friendly manner of someone who had been welcoming strangers for thirty years — sized up the three new arrivals in a single practised glance and produced cups without being asked.

"Travellers, is it. Come far?"

Conrad let the question hang just long enough to be polite, then drifted his attention across the room with the easy, unfocused look of a man admiring the rafters. He was not admiring the rafters. He was cataloguing.

A table of farmers in low conversation — the word *moathouse* drifting over before someone noticed the newcomers and the voices dropped. A merchant eating alone near the fire, well-dressed in the way that looked slightly wrong for a village this size, the way a costume looks slightly wrong when you know what the wearer usually wears. And at the bar, a man who had the worn, load-bearing look of a former soldier — not old, but carrying something that years of garrison life leave behind and civilian life never quite dissolves. He hadn't looked up when they came in.

Then Lance walked through the door, and the man looked up.

It wasn't hostile — the ranger clocked that immediately. It was something more careful: the way a man files a document rather than discards it. The ex-soldier's eyes tracked Lance's longbow, his gear, the particular quality of stillness he carried, and then returned to his drink. Deliberately.

Lance took his cup and found a wall to stand near. Michael drifted into the middle of whatever calculation occupied him. Conrad took the room.

---

## The Church of St. Cuthbert

The chapel was small and well-kept, its starburst symbol freshly painted over the door — the mark of St. Cuthbert, the Cudgel, patron of common sense and righteous retribution. Inside, a man named Terjon was trimming candles at the altar. He was perhaps forty, with the kind of face that had finished making its major decisions some time ago.

He turned when Thomas and Aldric entered. His eyes found Thomas's surcoat — the starburst worn openly, without ambiguity — and his expression shifted from neutral to carefully welcoming, the way a man moves when he recognises something he was half-hoping to see.

"Brothers," he said. The word carried the weight of *what brings you here* without quite asking it.

"I was sent by the order," Thomas said. "We've heard troubling news. Renewed humanoid activity. Whispers of something worse."

Terjon listened without interrupting. When Thomas finished, the canon was quiet for a moment — not surprised, exactly. More as though a suspicion had just received its confirmation.

"*Renewed,*" he said, with a particular weight on the word.

He gestured toward the pews, and they sat, and he told them what he knew.

The roads east of Hommlet had been unsafe for the better part of a season. Caravans raided. A farmstead burned, the family gone, no bodies found. The Keep on the eastern road was reporting humanoid sightings in the hills beyond its walls, more than they'd seen in years.

And the moathouse, south of the village, where the road dissolved into bog.

"Men have gone to look," Terjon said. "Not all of them have come back."

He paused. Looked at Thomas steadily.

"Bring your companions here in the morning. All of them. I'll tell you everything I know — but I'll tell it once, and I want the whole party to hear it together." He picked up his candle-snuffer. "In the meantime: whatever is in that moathouse, it is not merely bandits. The man who came back alone from the second party was a fighter of some experience. When I asked him what he saw, he wouldn't say. He left Hommlet the next morning."

He said the last part without drama. That was somehow worse.

"Sleep well, Paladin."

Aldric waited until they were outside before saying anything. "Cheerful fellow."

---

## What the Farmers Knew

Across the room, Conrad had made friends.

The farmer's name was Osric — weathered, careful, the kind of man who owned the field rather than worked it for someone else. He'd accepted Conrad's offered cup with the considering look of a man deciding whether a stranger was worth talking to. Conrad's response — something about the difference between being told there's a flock in the forest and seeing a single bird with your own eyes — had settled it.

"Three weeks back," Osric said, turning his cup, "I'm out before dawn checking the north fence. Something had been at it. I get to the treeline and there's torchlight moving through the wood. Not one torch. Six, seven. Moving south." He paused. "Moving quiet, for that many. Spaced wrong for men walking together. Like they didn't want to be seen from the road."

His broader son — Will, it emerged — couldn't help himself. "Tell them about the sounds."

Osric didn't look at him. "There were sounds. Not animals. Something that wasn't talking but wasn't quite not talking. Low. Carried further than it should have in the dark." He looked at Conrad. "I know what wolves sound like. I know what drunks sound like. This wasn't any of those things."

The younger son, perhaps sixteen, spoke quietly: "The Hendersons' dog went missing that same night. Big dog. Never found it."

"The torches were moving toward the moathouse road," Osric finished. He picked up his cup. "That's what I saw. Draw your own conclusions."

He glanced, just once, briefly, toward the man at the bar.

---

## The Man at the Bar

When Thomas entered the inn, Conrad got his attention and nodded towards the ex-soldier.

Thomas found the him on his barstool, nursing a drink at the careful pace of a man measuring his intake against the hours still to get through.

The soldier turned before Thomas reached him — sensing him, the way men with that kind of history do. His eyes went to the surcoat, and something in his face did something complicated. Not hostile. Not relieved. Something still deciding.

Thomas pulled out the stool beside him and sat. "My order sent me here because of troubling news — renewed humanoid activity, whispers of something worse. By the look of you, you've seen something. I'd rather know what I'm walking into than find out the hard way."

The ex-soldier looked at him for a long moment.

"Depends on whether you're planning to go there yourself," he said at last.

His name was Brandun. Former sergeant, King's road garrison, eastern reaches. He'd taken caravan work after leaving — good, quiet work, he said, with the flat affect of a man describing a life he wouldn't have again.

Three weeks past, his caravan had lost its outrider. A young man named Petyr. They'd backtracked and found his horse — just the horse. The tracks led toward the moathouse road.

"I followed them," Brandun said, "because that's what you do."

He stopped. Took a drink.

"I got close enough to see the moathouse. Close enough to see the gate had been cleared — the rubble moved, recently. Close enough to see torchlight inside the walls." He set his cup down and looked at Thomas directly. "And close enough to see what was standing guard at the gate."

He said the next words carefully, like a man who knows how they sound.

"It wasn't humanoids. It wasn't bandits. Dead things. Walking. Four of them that I could see, maybe more inside." He paused. "I left. Petyr didn't need me to find him anymore."

Beside Thomas, Aldric had gone very still.

"Your order sent you to look at humanoid activity," Brandun said. "That's not what's in that moathouse." He looked at the surcoat again. "Someone is in there running it. They've had weeks to settle in while everyone in this village has been pretending very hard it isn't their problem."

He finally drank.

"I've been sitting here four days asking myself what I'm going to do about it. I'm a retired sergeant with a sword and no particular desire to die in a ruin." A long pause. "But Petyr was nineteen."

Thomas held his gaze. "Undead are a scourge on the land. And if someone is raising them, that needs dealing with. We will find out what happened to your outrider."

The words landed simply, without theatre. Brandun looked at him for a moment — something in his face that might, in better light, have been mistaken for relief, but he'd been a sergeant too long to let it show cleanly.

Aldric put a hand briefly on Brandun's shoulder. Said nothing. Took it away.

Brandun reached into his coat and produced a folded piece of parchment. He'd had it ready. He'd drawn it days ago, and he'd been waiting for someone worth giving it to.

He spread it on the bar — a soldier's map, all distances and sight lines, unconcerned with artistry. The moathouse in its marsh, approachable only by a single raised track. The gatehouse, the walls, two tower positions, four X-marks at the gate. A note in cramped handwriting read *rubble cleared, recent.* The interior was blank. He hadn't gotten that far.

"Bog on three sides," he said. "One road in. They'll see you coming."

He folded the map and slid it to Thomas.

He turned back to his drink.

Aldric steered Thomas gently away by the elbow. "Undead guards," he murmured, "an organised presence, and a marsh on three sides." A pause. "I've had worse postings. I'm trying to remember when."

---

## The Evening's Reckoning

The party reconvened by the fire that night. The story assembled itself in pieces: Terjon's confirmation, Brandun's map, Osric's torchlight in the wood. Undead on the gate meant power — real power, not something a hedge witch stumbled into. Single approach road through marsh. Two previous parties had gone in. One came back short two members. The other hadn't come back at all.

"The undead concern me more than the humanoids," Aldric said, scraping the last of his supper. "Humanoids you can frighten off. The dead have nothing left to fear." He glanced at Thomas. "I can face them, given the right circumstances. But I'd rather know more before we walk into that marsh."

Michael had been thinking it through with the detached practicality of a man assessing tools. "I'm not useful against the dead directly," he said. "But if there are living things inside running this — I'm useful there."

Lance, who had said nothing, was still looking at Brandun's map. His finger traced the bog on three sides. The one road in.

"Could go at dawn," he said finally. "See what we're walking into."

The question hanging over the table was whether to gather more intelligence first — Terjon had promised more in the morning, and he'd mentioned a druid named Canoness Yether who knew the hills.

Aldric refilled his cup. "For what it's worth — and I recognise this is not my decision — I would very much like to speak to Terjon before walking into a marsh full of walking corpses." He took a drink. "One morning won't change what's in that ruin. It might change whether we survive going in."

Thomas agrees with Aldric - "I think we should talk with Terjon and the canoness in the morning. They might be able to give us more tactical information. Let's see about some rooms, get some rest, and head over to the church in the morning."

Conrad nods. "Agreed. No point in rushing into a marsh in the dark when there's a warm bed and information we haven't collected yet." He finishes his drink. "Besides, I want to know what Terjon is holding back."

Lance says nothing, which means he has no objection.

Michael is already thinking about spell preparation. 

Aldric stands, joints audible. "Excellent. A decision made sensibly and without anyone suggesting we leave immediately and travel by night." He picks up his bowl. "I'm going to remember this moment."

Gundigoot sets them up without fuss — two rooms, shared, reasonable rate. Four copper gets each of them a hot breakfast in the morning. He doesn't ask what they're planning and they don't tell him, which suits everyone.

The inn quiets down. Brandun is still at the bar when they head up, drinking at that same careful pace. He doesn't look up, but as Thomas passes he says, quietly, without turning:

"Dawn patrol at the moathouse changes around first light. If that matters to you."

He goes back to his drink.

Aldric, on the stairs, murmurs to Thomas: "He's been watching that ruin for four days from a warm inn and hasn't left. Either he's a coward, or he's waiting for something." A pause. "He doesn't seem like a coward."

The night passes without incident. Autumn cold, but the beds are decent. Lance is asleep in moments — the particular gift of men who have slept rough enough to appreciate anything with a roof. Conrad lies awake a little while, turning over the shape of things. Michael reads until the candle gutters.

---

## Morning: The Church, The Grove

Breakfast is hot and Gundigoot doesn't talk during it, which Aldric declares the mark of a truly civilised establishment.

Terjon was already up when they arrived — he was the kind of man who was always already up. He saw the full party this time, five where he'd been expecting two, and his expression recalibrated with the careful attention of someone taking new inventory.

"You brought everyone," he said. "Good."

He gestured to the pews, sat across from them, and folded his hands. He'd promised a full account, and he gave one.

The moathouse had once been the forward outpost of something called the Temple of Elemental Evil — real evil, not the word people used when they meant *unpleasant*, but the kind that cost lives to end. The Temple had been broken twenty years ago. The moathouse abandoned. Everyone had assumed it would stay that way.

Someone was using it again. Not as a temple — not yet. As a staging point, exactly as it had always been: supplies going in, undead at the gate as guards rather than ends in themselves, someone inside with enough power to put them there and keep them there. The surrounding land had been growing wrong for two months — humanoid activity in the eastern hills shifting from scattered raiding to something that looked like coordination. Goblinoid bands that had been fighting each other were suddenly moving together.

"Two things that should be separate problems," Terjon said, "that started at the same time. In my experience, that means they are not separate problems."

He looked at each of them in turn.

"One of my acolytes — a young man, reliable, not given to imagination — went to observe the moathouse a fortnight ago." He set down his candle-snuffer. "He came back and told me there was a light in the upper tower. Not torchlight." A pause. "He said it was green. I sent him to the capital three days later. For his own safety."

That sat in the air for a moment.

"I am a capable man," Terjon said, levelly. "I did not feel capable of this alone. Two adventuring parties passed through Hommlet this past month. One came back short two members. The other hasn't come back at all." He looked at Thomas. "I tell you this not to frighten you, but because I promised you honest counsel, and that is honest counsel."

He stood, reached into his robe, and produced a small token — a flat disc of worked bronze, St. Cuthbert's starburst stamped clean into the metal.

"Show this to the Castellan at the Keep. His name is Sherlane — a man of faith, compatible if not identical to our own. He'll give you what he can." A pause. "Tell him the moathouse is active again. He needs to hear it, and he'll believe it coming from you with that token."

Thomas took it.

Terjon looked at the party one last time. "There is also a Canoness named Yether who tends a grove on the north side of the village. She serves the Old Faith, but we share information when it matters. She knows the hills and woods between here and the Caves better than anyone living. Tell her I sent you — it will save twenty minutes of her deciding whether to trust you." A faint pause. "She doesn't trust quickly. It means she's paying attention."

He looked at them all — Aldric included.

"Fight well," he said. "Come back." Simply, the way a man says it when he has said goodbye to too many people who didn't.

Outside, the morning was crisp and bright. Aldric pulled his cloak tighter. "A druid who already knows we're coming, a Castellan who needs bad news, and a moathouse full of walking dead whose maker I haven't even seen yet." He considered the sky. "Brisk morning, though. That's something."

---

The grove was ten minutes north of the village, along a path that started as a proper track and gradually became something more like a suggestion. The trees here were old — older than the village, older than anyone could say — and the morning light came through them differently, quieter and more considered.

Lance slowed as they entered the treeline. Not from caution. From something else. He looked up at the canopy and his expression, usually shuttered, opened very slightly.

Michael noticed, and said nothing.

The woman in the clearing didn't turn around when they arrived. She was perhaps fifty, grey-streaked hair worn loose, tending a small fire in a stone ring with the focused attention of someone in the middle of a task that cannot be interrupted. She fed dried herbs into the flame, and a smell like pine resin and something sweeter rose from it. She let them wait for a moment that was slightly too long to be accidental.

"Five of you," she said, still without turning. "I expected four. The cleric is a late addition." A pause. "Sit, if you like. I'll be done in a moment."

Aldric looked at Thomas with an expression that said *Terjon warned us.*

The fire popped. She added one more handful, watched the flame change colour briefly — just a moment, a deep gold — and then stood and turned. Her eyes were sharp and brown and took in the party the way a hawk takes in a field.

"Yether," she said. "Terjon sent you — I know, I know. Sit down. I'll tell you what I know about the hills and you'll tell me what Terjon told you about the moathouse, because he knows more than he says, and I've been waiting three weeks for him to send someone I could talk to properly."

Conrad sat. Michael sat. Lance remained standing, moving to lean against one of the old trees at the clearing's edge — close enough, just not a bench-sitter. Aldric settled onto a root with a grateful exhale.

Yether looked at Thomas. "You first."

Thomas told her everything — Terjon's account of the moathouse, Brandun's map, the green light in the tower. She listened without interrupting, and when he finished she was quiet for a moment, looking at the fire.

"Green light," she said. Not a question. Something in her expression settled. "That's a name I was hoping not to hear connected to this."

She sat across from them on a worn log and told them what she had seen from the hills.

The natural order of the eastern territory had been wrong for two months — wrong in the way she meant it, not merely dangerous but *off.* Animals moving in patterns they shouldn't. Bird populations displaced from their usual valleys. Two of her markers in the hills had been disturbed — not destroyed, but moved carefully, by something that knew what they were and didn't want her to know it had been there.

"That last part concerns me most," she said. "Humanoids don't know what my markers are. Whatever disturbed them was either directed by someone who does, or is itself something that does."

The humanoid activity at the Caves had tripled. Species that had no natural instinct for cooperation were cooperating. She'd found offering sites in the hills — crude shrines, a consistent symbol on each, nothing from any surface tradition she recognised.

"Three weeks ago I tracked a figure moving through the northern wood at night," she continued. "Alone. No light. Moving with a stillness that wasn't human." She touched the standing stone briefly, unconsciously. "When it passed my oldest oak, the wood went quiet. Not the quiet of predator and prey. The quiet of something that doesn't belong here, and the forest knowing it."

She looked at the fire.

"I think there is one intelligence behind both — the moathouse and the Caves. Not human. Not local." She paused. "The green light in that tower is associated with a very old school of magic — necromantic, but not the crude kind. The sophisticated kind. The kind that requires learning, resources, and time." Another pause. "The kind a very particular sort of practitioner would use."

She stopped there. Looked at Michael.

"You're the magic-user," she said. "Does any of this mean anything to you yet?"

Michael met her gaze evenly. "Necromantic magic is outside my skills. I'm not equipped for the dead."

Yether nodded. "Honest answer. Good." She looked at him a moment longer. "Magic-users who survive long enough always find their skills expanding, whether they planned to or not." She said it the way a woman predicts weather. "For now — noted."

She turns back to the group.

"The point isn't that you need to understand the magic. The point is that whoever is in that moathouse is not an ambitious hedge priest who stumbled onto some old rituals. This is practiced. Deliberate." She pauses. "And patient. They've been there two months without overplaying their hand. That's discipline."

Aldric speaks up from his root. "Which means they're not finished yet. Whatever they're building toward."

Yether looks at him. "Correct. Which gives you a window." She stands, brushing earth from her knees. "Not an unlimited one."

She stood and moved to a small bundle near the standing stone, producing something — a sprig of dried herb, tied with red thread, a small carved wooden disc. She held it out to Lance, who was still at the treeline.

He looked at it without taking it.

"For the hills," she said simply. "Hang it visible on your pack. It won't protect you from everything. It will protect you from some things that would otherwise notice you." She paused. "The forest knows my markers. It will extend you a small courtesy."

Lance looked at it for another moment. Took it. Said nothing. Tied it to his pack with quiet efficiency.

Yether watched this with something that might have been approval. "Go to the Keep," she told them. "Approach the Caves carefully — small bites, not all at once. Come back to the moathouse when you're ready for what's inside, not just what's in front of it." She looks at Thomas. "The Castellan Sherlane is a good man. Tell him the coordinated activity in the hills is confirmed. He needs to increase his patrols on the eastern road."

She pauses. Looks at the party one more time — all of them, Aldric included, with those sharp brown eyes. "One more thing."

She said it quietly.

"The figure I tracked through the wood — when it passed, I caught a smell. Stone. Very deep stone. And something else — cold, but not winter cold. Old cold." She looked at the fire. "I've smelled that once before, thirty years ago, when I found a cave entrance in the deep hills that went further down than any cave should." She paused. "I sealed it and never went back."

She turned back to her fire. The audience, clearly, was over.

---

The path back through the trees felt slightly longer than it had going in. The morning light was full now, the village visible ahead. Nobody spoke for a moment.

Then Aldric said, quietly: "*Deep stone. Old cold.*" He pulled his cloak tighter. "I'm going to need more ale for this campaign than I budgeted for."

Conrad looked at Thomas. "The Keep?"

"The Keep," Thomas said.

---

## The Road East

They resupplied at the village trading post, a shop run by a broad, cheerful man named Rannos Davl who smiled too much and remembered everything. Conrad clocked him immediately as the kind of man who talked to multiple parties simultaneously and considered this simply good business.

When Conrad mentioned they were thinking of heading to the Keep, Rannos's smile didn't change. That was the tell. A genuine reaction would have shifted, even slightly. This one stayed exactly where it was.

"The Keep! Yes, yes, good road east, well-travelled, mostly safe in daylight." He's already moving to pull rope from a shelf. "Interesting places — well, there's the mill, of course, Hommlet's pride, and the new tower going up, you'll have seen that — Burne and Rufus, good men, building something substantial there." He sets the rope on the counter. "Beyond that, oh, the hills are lovely this time of year if you don't mind the walk. Some old ruins south of here but nobody goes there much. Marshy ground."

He says *marshy ground* with the practiced ease of a man who has described the moathouse as uninteresting many times.

Conrad keeps his face pleasantly neutral.

"Mostly what travellers want is the Keep," Rannos continues, tallying their purchases with quick hands. "Good garrison, fair prices, safe walls. You'll be comfortable there." He looks up with that fixed smile. "Staying long in Hommlet yourselves?"

*Where are you going and how long will you be gone* — dressed up nicely, but that's the question.

Nobody had mentioned the moathouse.

Conrad kept his face pleasantly neutral. "That tower going up — Burne and Rufus, was it? We might want to call on them. Learned men always know the interesting corners of a place." He let that sit just long enough. "We're not sure how long we'll be in the area. It all depends."

Rannos's eyes did a small, professional recalculation — quick, almost invisible — before the smile continued exactly as before. "Of course, of course. Burne's a magic-user, very learned, and Rufus a fine warrior. They're around most days." He tallied their purchases with quick hands. "Safe travels then — however long you stay, wherever you end up." He said it warmly. "Hommlet remembers its friends."

The door closed behind them.

They're twenty feet down the road before Lance says, quietly and without preamble: "He'll tell someone we're headed to the Keep."

Not *I think.* Not *maybe.* Just the flat assessment of a man stating observed fact.

Aldric, adjusting his pack: "The question is who he tells and how quickly."

Conrad glanced back once. Through the shop window, Rannos was already talking to a boy — young, maybe twelve. The boy nodded and slipped out the back door at a pace that was not quite a run.

"Hommlet remembers its friends," Conrad said pleasantly.

Thomas looked at him. The unspoken question.

"Doesn't change the plan," Conrad said. "We need what the Castellan knows, and it's not as though we could keep quiet forever in a village this size. Information flows. We work with that." A pause, his voice dropping half a register. "Besides, I've already told him we might call on Burne and Rufus at their tower. So as far as Rannos knows, we're sightseers who haven't decided anything."

Thomas nods. "Agreed. We go to the Keep as planned. Whatever Rannos tells whoever he tells — we're not hiding that we're here. We're investigators of the order, not spies."

"Speak for yourself," Conrad says pleasantly, and falls into step.

---

The road east was good for the first mile, then settled into the comfortable shabbiness of a route that saw regular use but not quite enough maintenance. Ruts from cart wheels, patched gravel, the occasional log-corduroy stretch over soft ground. The autumn countryside opened up around them — harvested fields, rougher pasture, then scrubby woodland as the land rose toward the eastern hills.

Lance moved differently out here. Nothing dramatic, just a loosening, a settling into himself that the inn's walls had made impossible. His eyes were in the treeline and the middle distance rather than fixed on the floor. He dropped back from the main group by habit — rear observation, natural spacing.

Aldric noticed. "He's better out here," he murmured to Thomas, not unkindly.

Two miles out, where a track headed south into heavier woodland, Lance stopped.

He crouched. Examined the verge without touching anything. Stood.

"Humanoids," he said quietly. "Goblinoids, I think. More than ten. Heading south." He looked at the treeline. "Recent. Last day, maybe less."

South was toward the moathouse road.

Aldric did his unhappy arithmetic again, silently.

Thomas looked at Lance. Lance looked at the track. A moment passed — the kind that contains a whole argument conducted without words. Then Lance stood, adjusted his pack, and walked east.  The set of his shoulders says he's filed it away — location, direction, approximate numbers, timeframe. It'll be there when they need it.

Conrad glanced at him as he passed. "Good call."

Lance said nothing, which was as close to *thank you* as he got.

---

At midday they stopped at a stream crossing. Lance went upstream to watch the road they'd come from. Old habit. Conrad sat beside Thomas on a flat rock and peeled a dried apple with his belt knife.

"Rannos," Conrad said, quietly. "He's not just a gossip. That boy — too fast, too practised. He's done that before." He ate a slice. "Someone in Hommlet is keeping track of who comes and goes and what they're interested in." A pause. "Worth knowing who he reports to. On the way back."

Aldric called from downstream: "I'd also like to know how he knew to describe the moathouse as *marshy ground* before anyone mentioned the moathouse."

A small silence.

Nobody had told Rannos they were investigating anything south of Hommlet.

Michael looked up from his thoughts. "We didn't tell him we were investigating anything south of Hommlet."

"No," Conrad agreed pleasantly. "We didn't."

Lance came back from upstream. Read the silence accurately. "What did I miss?"

---

## The Keep on the Borderlands

The Keep announced itself before it was visible — a thinning of trees, a widening of road, and then the smell of woodsmoke and horses and the particular density of a garrisoned settlement. Then the road curved and there it was: grey stone walls on a rise, a gatehouse, a flag snapping in the autumn wind. Guard detail on the walls — working soldiers, not ceremonial, scanning the treeline with professional attention. One of them clocked the party at two hundred yards and didn't look away.

At the gate, Thomas produced Terjon's bronze token and let it speak. One of the guards took it with a quiet word, disappeared inside at a pace that was faster than a stroll, and returned some minutes later with a man who walked like authority was simply how he moved.

Castellan Sherlane was perhaps fifty-five. Weathered, soldier-precise, with a priest's careful eyes — not St. Cuthbert, Thomas noted, but something orderly and lawful. *Compatible,* Terjon had said. The word seemed right.

He looked at the full party — five, taking in each in the efficient way of a man who had spent thirty years assessing groups. He noted Aldric's shared symbol with Thomas. He noted the charm on Lance's pack without comment. He noted Michael's lack of armour and the look of a man perpetually thinking.

"Come inside," he said. "All of you."

He turned without waiting to see if they followed.

---

The room off the main hall was practical — a table, chairs, a fire, and a map on the wall covered in annotations from several hands. Sherlane closed the door, sat, and folded his hands.

"Terjon sent you," he said. "Tell me what you know. All of it. Start with the moathouse."

Thomas told him. All of it — Terjon's account, Brandun's report, the green light, Yether's figure in the wood. He added Yether's message: the coordinated activity in the hills was confirmed. The patrols on the eastern road would need to increase.

Sherlane listened without moving. When Thomas finished, the Castellan was still for a moment — the stillness of a man absorbing something he'd hoped not to hear.

Then he exhaled. Once. Controlled.

"Green light," he said quietly. He looked at the table. "That's the third report of green light in that tower. I didn't credit the first. I half-credited the second." He looked up. "I credit three."

He stood and went to the map. Stood with his back to them for a moment, looking at it.

"The Temple of Elemental Evil," he said, to the map as much as to the room. "Twenty years ago, good people broke that temple at considerable cost. I was a younger man. I knew some of them." A pause. "The understanding was that it was finished. Broken thoroughly enough that rebuilding would take generations." He turned back. "Someone is apparently more patient than we gave them credit for."

He sat. Looked at Thomas directly.

"Terjon is right that these aren't separate problems. The timing is exact — moathouse reoccupied, humanoid coordination begins, patrol losses start. One intelligence. One plan." He paused. "And that plan has had two months of uninterrupted preparation while everyone decided it was someone else's problem."

Conrad, carefully neutral: "What's in the moathouse? Beyond what we've described."

Sherlane looked at him. Assessed — clocked the thief's gear, the precise question. Decided.

"Two levels. Ground floor, ruins — defensible if cleared, apparently now cleared. There is a lower level." He paused. "We don't know what's in the lower level. The party that came back came back from the ground floor. They didn't find the stairs down, or didn't look." He stopped again. "The report was incomplete."

The fire popped.

"Whoever can animate and command the dead doesn't stop at four gate guards," Sherlane said. He looked at the party — at Thomas's surcoat, at the wear on their gear, at the particular quality of men who were capable but had not yet been tested by anything like this. "You're new to this work. Not untrained. But new."

Not an accusation. A fact.

"I know," Thomas said.

Sherlane nodded slowly. "Then you know what I'm going to say."

"The Caves first," Thomas said.

"The Caves first," Sherlane confirmed. "Season yourselves. Come back to the moathouse when you can handle what's *in* it, not just what's in front of it. The Caves will also give you information, if you're paying attention. Whatever is coordinating the humanoids has reach into those hills. You may find evidence of it."

He stood. "You're welcome at the Keep as a base of operations. Standard rates for rooms and board. The smithy can do basic repairs." He glanced at Michael. "We have a library. Modest. You're welcome to it."

Michael straightened almost imperceptibly.

"One condition," Sherlane said. "Everything you find — everything relevant to the moathouse, the coordination, any evidence of who's behind this — you report to me. Not only to Terjon. To me."

Thomas agreed. He would keep Sherlane, Terjon, and Yether all informed. Conrad grumbled something under his breath about too many cooks. Sherlane's eyes moved to him briefly and away — the expression of a man who has heard that before.

He extended his hand. Thomas took it. A practical agreement, brief and firm.

At the door, Sherlane paused without turning. "The symbol Yether found on the offering sites. If you find it in the Caves — draw it exactly as you find it. Don't interpret it. Exact." He turned. "There are symbols associated with the old Temple that I have references for. If it matches anything I know, I want to know immediately."

## The Afternoon

The library was modest as promised — two shelves, forty volumes, a box of rolled maps, a smell of old parchment. Michael sat down in it and felt immediately comfortable.

He worked methodically through the military records and supply logs until, in the box of maps, he found what he needed: a rough survey of the eastern hills, annotated in multiple hands over many years. The Caves of Chaos marked in a ravine four miles east. The notes were illuminating in the way that notes left by people who have been somewhere dangerous tend to be illuminating.

*Multiple entrances — at least nine counted, possibly more concealed.*

*Ravine approach: exposed. Single file. Watched from above.*

And in older ink, faded: *Deeper caves connect. Have not mapped. Do not recommend.*

At the bottom, a single line in the most recent hand: *Shrine at ravine head. Unknown dedication. Do not approach.*

Michael found also a thin journal — a previous adventurer's account, water-damaged, its first twenty pages missing. What remained described the upper caves: goblin warrens, an orc stronghold, a cave bear den. A section that smelled of death, which the writer didn't elaborate on. The writing stopped mid-sentence.

He copied the map into his notes and brought both to share with the party.

---

Conrad drifted through the Keep's off-hours common spaces with the comfortable invisibility of a man who always looked as though he belonged. Soldiers talked freely enough around someone who bought a round and didn't ask direct questions.

The patrol losses three weeks ago had hit morale hard. Four men, two found. The soldiers who'd recovered them wouldn't talk about the condition they were in. That told Conrad what he needed to know.

There was also a merchant — arrived just before the party, hadn't left, name of Zelligar. Supposedly trading in preserved goods, though no trading had visibly occurred. He asked questions about road conditions east and south. Nobody thought much of it individually. Conrad thought plenty of it collectively.

And a young guard, third ale in, mentioned that two nights past he'd seen a light in the southern hills. Not fire. *Greenish, like. Lasted maybe a minute.* He'd woken his sergeant for it. The sergeant had seen nothing by the time he looked. The young guard had gotten latrine duty and was still aggrieved about it.

Conrad filed all three items and went to find the rest of the party.

---

Lance was out the gate and into the treeline within minutes, the Keep's walls falling away behind him, his breathing evening out the way it did when walls stopped pressing on him.

He worked east and south, off the road, through the late afternoon woods. Yether's charm sat visible on his pack. Twice, small animals that should have fled paused a moment longer than natural before moving off. He noted this without surprise.

The hills rose toward the Caves. He didn't go all the way — too far alone, too late in the day — but he got high enough to see the ravine mouth from a distance. A dark gap in the hillside. The approach was exactly as the map annotations had warned: open ground, visible from above, single file through scrub.

He saw smoke. Thin and careful, rising from inside the ravine. Someone had a cooking fire. Someone was home.

On the way back, he cut south and found the goblinoid trail from the morning — or its continuation. It had joined a second trail here. Two groups, converging, heading south together.

Toward the moathouse road.

He marked the junction in his memory with the exactness he marked everything, and turned back toward the Keep as the light failed.

---

Thomas had the Keep's small chapel to himself for most of the afternoon. He prayed, and worked oil into his chainmail checking each link, and thought about what Terjon had said, what Sherlane had said, what the green light meant. He thought about Brandun, and Petyr, and the two guards who had been found, and the two who hadn't.

He pressed his hands together and held his faith close to him, feeling the warmth of it move through him the way heat moves through stone. It was a small and private ritual, a checking of what remained.

Near the end of the afternoon, Aldric appeared in the chapel doorway. He looked at Thomas for a moment, then came in without being invited. Sat in the pew behind him. Didn't say anything for a while.

Then: "I've been a cleric twenty years. I've seen dark things." A pause. "What Yether described — deep stone, old cold — that's not the moathouse. That's something *using* the moathouse."

He was quiet again.

"I want you to know I'm not going anywhere. Just. Wanted to say it."

He got up and left before Thomas could respond.

---

## Evening: What They Knew

The party reconvened over supper in the common hall. Soldiers at other tables. The merchant Zelligar visible across the room, eating alone, watching nothing in particular with the practised attention of a man watching everything.

Conrad set down his cup. "Report."

Michael laid out his copied map and the damaged journal. Lance described the ravine smoke and the converging trails to the south. Conrad told them about the patrol deaths, the suspiciously incurious merchant, and the young guard's green light sighting two nights past.

The picture that assembled itself was not comforting.

Thomas said: "We need to be careful in those Caves. One at a time, no heroics, retreat when necessary. And we keep our eyes open for anything that connects the Caves to what's happening at the moathouse."

Conrad nodded. "One cave at a time. Clear it, pull back, rest, go again. We don't get greedy." He looked at Michael's map. "Nine entrances minimum means nine potential problems. We pick the smallest-looking first."

"Goblins," Lance said. He'd been studying the map since Michael put it down. "Start with goblins. Weakest first, learn the ground, work up."

Michael tapped the damaged journal. "The writer mentions the caves connect deeper in. We don't go deeper until we're ready." He paused. "The shrine at the ravine head — we leave that alone for now."

"*Definitely* leave that alone," said Aldric, with feeling.

Conrad turned his cup slowly. "The merchant. Zelligar." He didn't look across the room — professional habit. "Four days here, no visible trading, asks about roads south and east, arrived just before us." He paused. "Could be coincidence."

"Could be Rannos's boy delivered a message faster than we walked," Lance said.

Another silence.

"We don't show him we've noticed," Conrad said. "We act like travelling adventurers heading to the Caves for coin and glory." Almost a smile. "Which is essentially what we are."

Thomas looked at the group. "We tell Sherlane about Zelligar in the morning. Quietly, before we leave. Let him watch the man while we're gone." He paused. "And the green light sighting — he needs to know that too. His soldier saw it two nights ago. That's recent."

"Very recent," Michael agreed quietly.

Aldric finished his ale. "So. Morning — brief Sherlane, head east, find the smallest cave entrance, try not to die." He set down his cup. "I've had worse plans. Not many, but some."

Lance was already looking at the door — not restlessly, just ready. The woods were out there and the Caves were in the hills and there was work to do.

Conrad glanced once more at Zelligar across the room. The merchant was eating calmly, looking at nothing.

Looking at nothing very carefully.

"Early start," Conrad said. "Before our friend over there has his breakfast."

---

The party turned in. The Keep settled into its nighttime rhythms — watch changes, the distant clank of the smithy banking its fire, a horse shifting in the stable. The autumn cold pressed at the narrow windows.

Somewhere south, in a ruined moathouse deep in a marsh, a green light burned in an upper tower.

The Caves of Chaos were four miles east.

Morning would come early.
